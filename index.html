What I think

Games made a promise nobody has cashed yet.

Your brain has always been a story engine. It takes raw experience and converts it into meaning, constantly, whether you ask it to or not. That's the mechanism every storyteller has ever tapped into: novels, film, theater. You fill in what's not on the page. You convince yourself you felt something that wasn't explicitly there. The story engine does the work.

"As a player, to be an active participant in the story being told." That's the promise. Forty years on, it's still open.

The industry has solved almost everything around that promise. Graphics are photoreal. Worlds are enormous. Distribution reaches billions. But the promise itself? There are no truly great video game characters yet. And I'll stand behind that provocation.

Characters are the critical material of story. Agency is what makes them real. Hamlet chooses, and the choices cost him something, and you feel it because you're occupying the same moral space. In games, player agency and character agency eat each other. Give the player full control and the character becomes a puppet. Author the character's choices and the player becomes a spectator. Most games bridge that gap by leaning on the story engine: players fill in enough meaning that it feels like more than it is.

The industry has a name for the symptom: ludonarrative dissonance. Naming it isn't solving it.

There are actually two problems on this path. The first is awareness: building games that track what you do, understand what it meant, and use it to produce meaningfully different outcomes. Players have been asking for persistence more than branching all along. They remember the betrayal. They don't remember the number of systems running underneath.

The second, longer problem is shared agency: characters that hold their own life and will inside the same space as the player. That's the Hamlet test. Nobody has built that yet.

AI is the first tool that makes both problems tractable. Almost everyone is pointing it at production efficiency. I'm interested in something harder.

What happens when a story can finally talk back?
